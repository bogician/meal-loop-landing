# PRD Quality Review — MealLoop Marketing Site v2

## Overall verdict

This is a launch-grade PRD that punches well above the stakes of a single landing page: it has a real thesis (scale capability *up* while scaling noise *down*), a clean FR-to-feature-to-SM spine, and unusually disciplined testable consequences on nearly every FR. The main risks are localized, not structural — a handful of FR consequences hide adjectives ("crisply," "gracefully," "legible") that stories will trip on, and the App Store CTA being a placeholder means the north-star metric (SM-1) can't actually be validated at launch, which the PRD acknowledges but doesn't fully resolve. Two named UJs carry the consumer shape adequately, though both protagonists converge on the same path and the second persona ("Mark") does little independent work.

## Decision-readiness — strong

The PRD makes decisions and states them as decisions. The central bet is named in §1 ("scale the site up in capability while scaling *down* in noise") and the five-pillar framing (§1) is a real prioritization, not a feature dump. Trade-offs are surfaced honestly rather than smoothed: the addendum names what was given up — next-i18next rejected as "Pages-Router-oriented and legacy" (addendum i18n), Plausible "kept as fallback" with the explicit cost ("paid or self-hosted and an extra integration"), and the cookieless-analytics reality that "a consent banner is not strictly mandatory *for analytics alone*" but ships anyway for GDPR posture. That last point is the kind of tension a reviewer would push on, and it's met head-on rather than dodged.

The Open Questions (§8) are genuinely open and owned (e.g. OQ-3 persisted-preference mechanism, OQ-5 second native uk reviewer) — not rhetorical questions answered in the next sentence. The single `[NOTE FOR PM]` at §4.3 (confirm `brand-logo.svg` is "final/locked before deriving the favicon + OG set, to avoid regenerating later") lands at a real sequencing tension, not a safe checkpoint.

### Findings
- **medium** North-star metric is unmeasurable at launch (§7 SM-1, §8 OQ-1) — SM-1 is the declared north-star but the app "is not yet on the store," so SM-1 degrades to "track Coming-soon CTA interactions as conversion-intent and watch the trend." A decision-maker green-lighting v2 should know the headline success signal is a proxy until an external dependency (OQ-1, owner Bogdan, "Revisit: at app submission") lands. *Fix:* state explicitly what launch-phase success looks like for SM-1 in proxy form (e.g. an intent-rate floor or a directional bar) so the metric is actionable before the listing flips.

## Substance over theater — strong

Content is earned, not furniture. Personas (Olena, Mark in §2.3) each map to concrete requirements — UJ-1 "Realizes the i18n, mobile, and CTA-state requirements," UJ-2 "Realizes the SEO/performance, mobile, analytics, and CTA requirements" — and the PRD stays under the four-persona ceiling. There is no innovation-theater differentiation section; the product's restraint ("sells less," §1/§11) is treated as a constraint, not a marketing claim. The NFRs in §10 carry product-specific bounds (LCP ≤ 2.5s, CLS ≤ 0.1, INP ≤ 200ms, ≥44px touch targets) rather than "must be fast/secure." Counter-metrics (§7 SM-C1–C3) are real anti-goals — SM-C1 explicitly refuses to optimize time-on-page upward ("a visitor who converts to the store in 30 seconds is a success"), which is the correct tell that the metrics validate the thesis rather than measure activity.

### Findings
- **low** "Mark" persona does limited independent work (§2.3 UJ-2) — Olena (UJ-1) already covers mobile, CTA-state, and locale; Mark adds the shared-link entry and the 40-second decision but otherwise traverses the same hero→screenshots→loop→badge path. Not theater (he carries the SEO/performance + analytics emphasis), but the second UJ is thinner than the first. *Fix:* optional — either sharpen Mark's distinct stress (e.g. mobile-data performance budget) or accept one rich UJ plus a lighter variant.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a thesis and the features serve it. §1 frames v2 as a maintainability + discoverability + trust upgrade to an existing one-shot page "without changing what it *is*," and every feature group (§4.1–4.7) is traceable to one of the five pillars. Prioritization follows the thesis rather than ease-of-build: legal pages (§4.6) are sequenced as a dependency because they "unblock FR-20 and (downstream) App Store submission," and brand assets (§4.3) "unblock FR-10" — these are thesis-driven dependency chains, not "what's easy first." Success metrics validate the bet: SM-2 (organic discoverability) validates the SEO pillar, SM-3 (bilingual reach) validates i18n, SM-4 (mobile CWV) validates the mobile pillar. The MVP scope is coherently an *experience/platform* upgrade of a known artifact, and §6.2 + §5 keep that scope honest. This does not read as a backlog with headings.

## Done-ness clarity — adequate

This is the dimension to be unforgiving on, and the PRD is mostly excellent here: nearly every FR carries an explicit **Consequences (testable)** block, and most are genuinely verifiable — FR-1 "`/en` and `/uk` each render the full landing page in the correct language," FR-5 "`/uk` emits `lang=\"uk\"`," FR-7 "`/uk` lists `en`, `uk`, and `x-default` alternates," FR-11 "One `<h1>` per page," FR-23 "Changing `APP_STORE_URL` ... updates every CTA instance with no other edit." That is a high bar held across 24 FRs. The deductions are for a residue of adjective-bounded consequences that stories cannot turn into a pass/fail check without inventing the threshold themselves.

### Findings
- **high** Unbounded quality adjectives in consequences (§4.3 FR-12, §4.4 FR-16, FR-17, §4.7 FR-24) — several "testable" consequences are not testable as written: FR-12 "renders crisply at nav and footer sizes," FR-16 "degrade gracefully and remain legible on small viewports," FR-17 "type remain legible" / "meet the legibility baseline in §10," FR-24 "calm Coming-soon affordance." "Crisply," "gracefully," "legible," and "calm" have no verifiable condition. FR-17 forwards legibility to §10, but §10 Accessibility only says "sufficient contrast" and never states a minimum mobile font size, so the chain dead-ends. *Fix:* replace adjectives with bounds — e.g. FR-17 "body ≥16px, headings per the §10 scale"; FR-16 "no layout shift > CLS 0.1 and no clipped/overlapping content at 320–430px widths"; FR-12 "vector mark, no rasterization blur at 1x/2x." Add the mobile font floor to §10 so FR-17's forward reference resolves.
- **medium** Consent-gating consequence is conditional on an unresolved choice (§4.5 FR-20) — "Non-essential tracking does not run until consent is given (where consent is required for the chosen tooling — see addendum)." Because the addendum concludes Vercel Analytics is cookieless and "a consent banner is not strictly mandatory," the FR's primary acceptance condition may evaluate to vacuously true. An engineer cannot tell whether "analytics must be gated behind consent" is a requirement or not. *Fix:* pick the posture in the FR (gate analytics load behind consent: yes/no) rather than deferring to a conditional; OQ-3 and the addendum already lean far enough to decide.
- **low** "Trending up" / "trend" metrics lack a window (§7 SM-2, SM-3, SM-1 proxy) — "trending up after launch" and "watch the trend" have no measurement window or comparison baseline. Acceptable for secondary discoverability metrics, but worth a defined review cadence so they're checkable. *Fix:* name a window (e.g. "30/60-day post-launch").

## Scope honesty — strong

Omissions are explicit and do real work. §5 Non-Goals is substantive and specific (no blog/CMS, no waitlist/email, no dark-mode toggle, no locales beyond en/uk, no support surfaces, no net-new logo), and these are reinforced at the point of temptation — e.g. the `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` on FR-24 ("email/waitlist capture on the Coming-soon state ... no email collection") sits exactly where a reader might silently assume otherwise. §6.2 de-scopes real screenshots, OS-driven dark mode, extra structured-data types, and RUM dashboards openly, each with a `[NOTE FOR PM]` or rationale. The §9 Assumptions Index is present and each entry is tagged inline. Open-items density (5 Open Questions + 6 indexed assumptions + 2 `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts) is proportionate to a genuine green-light-to-build PRD — none of the open items block the core build; the one external blocker (App Store listing) is correctly flagged as a dependency, not hidden.

## Downstream usability — strong

This PRD is chain-top (feeds UX → architecture → stories), so traceability matters, and it holds up. The §3 Glossary is present and the domain nouns (Locale, Message catalog, App Store CTA, Coming-soon state, Brand mark vs. Wordmark, hreflang, Consent banner) are used consistently across FRs, UJs, and SM definitions. IDs are contiguous and unique: FR-1–FR-24 with no gaps, UJ-1/UJ-2, SM-1–SM-4 plus SM-C1–C3. Cross-references resolve and use named anchors (FR↔SM via "Validates FR-…", features↔UJs via "Realizes UJ-…", FRs↔NFRs via "§10," voice via "§11/UX-DR18"). Sections stand alone — each feature group has its own Description, FRs, and Out-of-Scope, and references go through Glossary terms rather than "see above." The §9 roundtrip is clean. The only soft spot is the FR-17→§10 forward reference noted above (the §10 legibility bound doesn't exist), which is a Done-ness issue but also a downstream-extraction snag.

## Shape fit — strong

The shape matches the product. This is a consumer-facing acquisition site, so named-protagonist UJs are load-bearing — and the PRD supplies them (Olena, Mark) with entry state, path, climax, resolution, and edge cases, correctly weighting the experience dimensions (i18n detection, mobile, CTA-state, performance). It is not over-formalized: there's no UJ sprawl, no fabricated stakeholder matrix, and the builder-as-PM JTBD (§2.1 "Ship and maintain without re-pasting strings") is acknowledged without being inflated into its own persona journey. It is not under-formalized: a consumer product with this much UX surface would be hollow without UJs, and they're here. The brownfield reality (v1 already live on Vercel) is handled accurately — §1 names the specific v1 gaps (English-only, no analytics, placeholder logo, dead legal links) and §4 frames each pillar as a delta from existing code (e.g. FR-12 "The placeholder `LoopMark` is replaced," FR-22 footer links "currently point to `#`"). New vs. existing is distinguished throughout.

## Mechanical notes

- **Glossary drift:** none material. Terms are used with consistent casing and number across the document. "Brand mark" vs. "Wordmark" is explicitly disambiguated in §3 and the distinction is respected in FR-12.
- **ID continuity:** clean. FR-1 through FR-24 contiguous with no gaps or duplicates; UJ-1/UJ-2; SM-1–SM-4 and counter-metrics SM-C1–SM-C3. All "Validates FR-…" and "Realizes UJ-…" cross-references resolve to existing IDs.
- **Assumptions Index roundtrip:** consistent. The six §9 entries correspond to inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tags (§2.1 uk audience, §3 default locale ×2, §4.1/FR-2 detection, §7/SM-1 ≥10% target, §6.2 screenshots, §4.5/FR-20 consent scope) and §10's standalone CWV `[ASSUMPTION]`. Minor: the §10 CWV-thresholds assumption ("standard 'good' CWV thresholds adopted as targets") is tagged inline but not surfaced in the §9 index — index it for completeness.
- **UJ protagonist naming:** both UJs have named protagonists (Olena, Mark) carrying context inline; no floating UJs.
- **Cross-doc references:** the PRD points to external sources (`../MealLoop/.../DESIGN.md`, `microcopy-voice.md` UX-DR18, `brand-logo.svg`, `AppIcon.png`) and the addendum. These were not independently verified against the iOS repo during this review; FR-12/FR-13/FR-14 and §11 depend on those paths being accurate — worth a one-time confirmation that the cited asset paths exist as written.
- **Required sections:** all expected sections for a launch-grade consumer PRD are present (Vision, Target User/JTBD/UJs, Glossary, Features+FRs, Non-Goals, MVP Scope, Success Metrics + counter-metrics, Open Questions, Assumptions Index, NFRs, Tone/Brand constraints, Privacy/Compliance guardrails).
